Inclusive Design and Accessibility

Practical Fixes for Common WCAG Failures

Presented by Erik Kroes

WCAG - Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

1. Keyboard

Pressing the Tab key should take you to every interactive element on your site. Shift + Tab should move focus in the reverse direction.

Common issues include:

  • No skip link for quickly jumping to main content
  • Uncontrolled or invisible focus states
  • Improper use of elements like <div role="button"> without correct keyboard behavior

2. Contrast

Even in bright environments (like in the sun), poor contrast affects everyone. This is not just about accessibility, but also about usability.

Modern browsers now include built-in contrast tools. You no longer need external apps. The contrast ratio should be at least 3:1 for larger text, and ideally 4.5:1 for body text.

Frequent problems include:

  • Low-contrast placeholders in form fields
  • Inaccessible branding colors
  • Poorly styled states like hover, active, or disabled

3. Alternatives

Always provide alt text for images, meaningful labels for icons and form fields, and transcripts for audio/video. This ensures content is usable with screen readers and accessible technologies.

4. & Sizing

Allow users to resize text and layouts. Avoid fixed pixel sizes. Use relative units like em, rem, or percentages to ensure responsive, accessible layouts.
